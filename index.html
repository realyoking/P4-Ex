Reading Passage 1: Ocean Park

Last Sunday, my family visited Ocean Park in Hong Kong. It was a bright and sunny day. We rode the cable car up the hill. From the top, we saw the sparkling sea and tall green trees all around us. The park was busy with happy visitors.

First, we went to see the giant pandas. They were eating fresh bamboo and rolling playfully on the grass. The pandas looked very cute and relaxed. My little sister wanted to hug one, but the sign said it was not allowed.

Next, we watched the exciting dolphin show. The smart dolphins jumped high out of the water and played with colourful balls. They waved their tails at the crowd. Everyone clapped and cheered because the show was wonderful.

We also visited an exhibition about ocean pollution. There were pictures of dirty beaches and animals hurt by plastic rubbish. The guide explained that we must throw away rubbish correctly and recycle to protect sea turtles, fish and other creatures.

At the end of the day, we bought souvenirs and took many photos. I felt tired but very happy. The visit taught me to care for nature and keep our environment clean. I hope we can return to Ocean Park soon.

Reading Passage 2: The Science Museum

Our school organized a trip to the Hong Kong Science Museum last Friday. I was extremely excited because I love learning about how things work. When we walked in, the first thing we saw was the giant Energy Machine. It is the tallest machine in the museum, and we watched a ball drop down long tracks, making loud sounds.

Later, we went to the electricity room. We safely touched a special silver ball that made our hair stand straight up! Everyone laughed at how funny we looked. The guide told us this is called static electricity.

Before leaving, we watched a 3D movie about space exploration. We learned that astronauts float in space because there is zero gravity. The trip was fantastic, and I learned a lot of new science facts.

Reading Passage 3: The History of Dim Sum

Dim sum is a traditional Chinese meal made up of small plates of dumplings and other snack dishes. The tradition of eating dim sum began hundreds of years ago along the ancient Silk Road. Travelers needed a place to rest, so teahouses opened along the road.

At first, people only drank tea. But later, doctors discovered that drinking tea with food helps digestion, so teahouses began serving small snacks. This was the beginning of "yum cha," which means "drinking tea."

Today, dim sum is very famous in Hong Kong. Families love to gather on Sunday mornings to eat har gow (shrimp dumplings), siu mai, and egg tarts. It is not just about the delicious food; it is a time for families to talk and share their week together.
